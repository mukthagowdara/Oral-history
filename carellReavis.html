Mr. Carell Reavis

Early Years

Mr. Carell Reavis was born on May 11, 1923 in Brunswick County Virginia as the 5th child among a family of 16. As a young boy he grew up on a farm that his grandmothers’ father inherited part of the plantation. Due to lack of transportation, he didn’t attend high school but attended the grammar school. He worked fulltime on another farm that grew tobacco, cotton, corn, wheat, sugarcane, livestock and more. By the age of 7 depression hit, and due to confiscation of land due to his grandmother’s passing, his family had to move another place with the family working for $15.00 per month.

Military Service

At the age of 16, Mr.Reavis joined the Civilian Conservation Corps(CCC) started by Roosevelt in 1933. He was paid $30 monthly of which $25 was sent home to the family. He next joined the US Navy at Yorktown, VA where his work included filling bombs/warheads for torpedoes. At the age of 19, he got drafted into Marine Corps and when war broke reported to Montford Point in 1943.

Struggles at camp Lejeune - Montford Point was a swamp area part of Camp Lejeune near the town of Jacksonville. There were no barracks and soldiers had to build huts themselves out of cardboard material. To stay warm they had to heat coals with potbellied stoves called skull buckets which was to be shared by 16 people in a hut. Snakes and bears were frequent visitors. Food was limited to 1 meat, 2 slices of bread and 1 glass milk served in regular mess hall. The marines were not supposed to gain weight because they only had a certain size of uniform, size 6, and would be put out if one needed anything larger than that.

Training - Everybody entered the Marine Corps as a Private. Everybody had to be trained and go through bootcamp. There were no Negro non-commissioned officers. All non-commissioned officers were white and training was given by them. The first six negros trained would be drilled instructors, NCO’s sergeants. After 8 weeks off training they were shipped to Pacific to the 51 and 52 Defense Battalion. He did supply work, depot handling equipment.

Mr.Reavis worked at duty stations for twenty one years, every year at a different station. He served at Naval Stations in Philadelphia, Hawaii, etc. and was the drill instructor for Marine corps recruit depot(MCRD), and was a rifle range coach. His duties also included inspection of ammunition which were manufactured from World War I period, dumped world war I gas equipment’s wearing a suit of asbestos. Infantryman could be out 2 months then come back for a week or two and then back out again. Everyone had to be an infantryman at one time. In 2012, he was awarded the Congressional Gold Medal, the nation’s highest civilian honor, “for outstanding perseverance and courage that inspired social change in the Marine Corps.”

Family life while in Military – At the age of 20 he got married, but during that time spouse was not allowed to go with the soldier due to monetary issues and less female force in the military at the time. Back then a soldier had to be at least a sergeant to get married to be able to provide for his family with a salary of $72 per month. Soldiers were required to write a letter every day to family and during war, the mail was censored so by the time loved ones received mail more than half of the letter would be erased due to privacy issues.

Post Military Life

After retirement Mr. Reavis received his high school diploma. He has served on several boards including Francis Parker School- Pacific Coast Bank. He was the first director of the MLK parade there. He has ventured into being a barber, mason, and realtor. He was a Member of Montford Point Marines Association start 1965. He could not join the American Legion because Blacks were not allowed –one of the reasons the Montford Point Marines Association formed. He has served as the second President Montford Point Association in San Diego-formed in 1978 and served as the Advisory to County Supervisory.
